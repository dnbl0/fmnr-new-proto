What is FMNR?

Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration (FMNR) is a low-cost, sustainable land restoration technique. It involves regenerating and managing trees and shrubs from existing stumps, roots and seeds to combat poverty, increase food production and enhance resilience to climate extremes.

Because a significant proportion of the world's degraded agricultural land was cleared using methods that leave living roots in the ground, FMNR is often an ideal — and fast — way to start regreening.

How is FMNR different from planting trees?

Rather than raising and planting new seedlings, FMNR regenerates the living stumps, roots and seeds that already exist in the soil — the “underground forest”. This makes it far cheaper, faster and more resilient than conventional tree planting, because the root systems are already established.

Farmers can implement FMNR without external inputs or expensive equipment, which is what makes it so scalable.

What are the five steps of FMNR?

FMNR follows five simple steps: Find — survey the land and select promising stumps and species; Prune — select the strongest stems and remove the rest; Protect — guard regrowth from fire, livestock and weeds; Grow — prune regularly so the main stems grow strong; and Utilise — manage the trees for the firewood, shade, fruit, fodder and other benefits the community needs.

How much does FMNR cost?

Very little. FMNR requires no expensive inputs or specialised equipment — only knowledge, basic tools and the labour that already exists within farming communities. This is why it can spread so rapidly from farmer to farmer.

Where is FMNR practised?

FMNR began in Niger in the 1980s and has since spread to more than 30 countries across Africa, Asia and the Pacific. World Vision alone has seen FMNR adopted by individuals, communities and more than 2,000 partners worldwide.

What impact does FMNR have?

In southern Niger, FMNR helped regenerate millions of hectares of farmland — a transformation so vast it is visible from space. Farmers there now leave an average of 40 trees per hectare (some as many as 150), benefiting from increased income, improved crop yields and more fodder for livestock. Wind speeds have dropped and, in some regions, water tables have risen.

How is World Vision involved?

For over two decades, World Vision has led the global effort to scale FMNR. It has trained almost one million farmers and helped regenerate nearly one million hectares of degraded land. World Vision is committed to restoring 27 million hectares and reaching 8.1 million children over 10 years, and to equipping partners with the knowledge and resources to spread FMNR further.

How can I get involved?

Anyone can join the global FMNR movement — by learning the technique, spreading awareness, taking part in local projects, or supporting initiatives that directly implement FMNR. These efforts help restore degraded lands and build sustainable futures for children and their communities.
