About FMNR · Scaling Projects

From One Farmer To Five Million Hectares.

FMNR began with a single change of mind in Niger and has spread, farmer to farmer, across regions, landscapes and nations. This is how a low-cost technique becomes a movement at scale.

Built to spread

Why FMNR Scales Where Planting Programmes Fail

Conventional reforestation — raising seedlings in nurseries and planting them out — repeatedly failed in the drylands. FMNR works with what is already in the ground: living rootstock, stumps and seeds. That is what lets it move from a single farm to a whole landscape at almost no cost.

Low cost

No Inputs, No Nurseries

FMNR requires no external inputs or expensive equipment. A significant proportion of degraded agricultural land was cleared by methods that leave roots in the ground — making regreening immediately possible at scale.

Farmer-led

Spread Farmer To Farmer

In Niger the practice spread largely from one farmer to the next over a 20-year period. Visible results — better crops growing amongst the trees — did the convincing, with little investment from government or NGOs.

Adaptable

Tailored To Each Landscape

Communities tailor FMNR to their own needs — available species, local climate, soil and crop conditions — so the same core technique takes root across deserts, croplands and highlands alike.

01

It Starts In Niger

In the early 1980s Niger's agricultural zone was turning to desert. During a country-wide famine, a food-for-work programme required communities to protect trees on their farmland — and for the first time some 500,000 trees were left standing across a whole district. Crops grew better amongst the trees, and the idea took hold.

  • 500,000 trees protected in a single district
  • A change in mindset, not a planting campaign
  • Farmers saw better crops within one season
  • The technique outlived the project that started it

02

Five Million Hectares, Visible From Space

Over the following 20 years FMNR spread from farmer to farmer until five million hectares of farmland in Niger had been re-vegetated — a transformation so large it is visible in satellite imagery from 1975 compared to 2005. This happened in one of the world's poorest countries, moving FMNR from a "project" to a "movement".

  • 5 million hectares re-vegetated in Niger
  • 200 million trees regenerated across the Sahel
  • Desertification halted, deforestation reversed
  • Achieved with minimal forestry-sector investment

03

Catalysing New Regions

From the Sahel, FMNR has been deliberately catalysed across new landscapes — from the Humbo regreening project in Ethiopia to the Uganda FMNR Catalyst Project. The pattern repeats: train, demonstrate, let visible results drive adoption, then step back as communities carry the practice forward themselves.

  • Humbo, Ethiopia — degraded hillsides restored
  • Uganda FMNR Catalyst Project — household clusters
  • Active across 20+ countries and 5 continents
  • Farmer-led spread designed in from day one
