Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration

Regreening starts from the roots

Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration (FMNR) is a low-cost, sustainable land restoration technique. It involves regenerating and managing trees and shrubs from stumps, roots and seeds to combat poverty, increase food production and enhance resilience to climate extremes.

Farmers can implement FMNR without external inputs or expensive equipment, making it a scalable approach for restoring degraded land. A significant proportion of Earth's degraded agricultural land was cleared using methods that leave roots in the ground — making FMNR an ideal way to start regreening.

What is FMNR

From Deforestation to Restoration.

Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration is a low-cost technique that fights poverty, hunger and climate change by allowing trees to regenerate naturally on farms. It's not about planting new trees — the roots are already there, waiting.

How it works

The five steps of FMNR

Communities tailor FMNR to their own needs, resources and climate — but the practice always follows the same five simple steps.

Step 01

Find

Rally the community to create a realistic wish list of tree species — firewood, shade, fruit. Survey the land to find which species are present and identify indigenous shrubs with extensive root systems.

Step 02

Prune

Pruning is a key principle of FMNR and is essential for its success. Select the strongest stems on each stump and carefully remove the rest so the tree can grow tall and strong.

Step 03

Protect

Protect regrowing trees from fire, livestock and competing weeds. Ideally, exclude livestock for six months to a year so trees grow strong enough to withstand grazing pressure.

Step 04

Grow

New branches and shoots keep growing, so prune them every two to six months. This helps the main stems grow stronger and faster. Consistent care is key.

Step 05

Utilise

FMNR trees serve many purposes based on what the community needs. Members have the freedom to tailor FMNR to local climate, soil and crop conditions, using available resources.

Impact seen from space

Niger, 1975 → 2005

In southern Niger, farmer-led regeneration transformed barren land into productive, tree-filled farmland — a change so vast it is visible from space.

Before FMNR — 1975

Farmers believed a good farmer was a "clean" farmer who cut down all farm trees and burned crop residue. As trees belonged to the government, there was no incentive to protect them — so theft was common and many farmers cut their own trees for the wood.

After FMNR — 2005

Today, farmers leave an average of 40 trees per hectare — some as many as 150 — on their farmland. They benefit from increased income, improved crop yields and more fodder for livestock. Wind speeds have reduced and, in some regions, water tables have risen.
