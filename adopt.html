What is FMNR?

Your land already holds the answer

Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration is a low-cost, farmer-driven technique that works with nature, not against it. Instead of planting new trees, FMNR protects and manages the regrowth of trees and shrubs from existing root systems already in your soil.

Regrowth

Use Living Roots

Find existing stumps, roots and shrub regrowth, then protect the strongest shoots so trees can return quickly.

Knowledge

Learn by Practice

The technique requires no expensive inputs. It depends on observation, pruning, protection and consistent management.

Results

Build Resilience

Used across dozens of countries, FMNR restores degraded farmland, improves soil health, increases yields and helps communities withstand drought.

The Process

Four steps to regeneration

Each FMNR step builds on the last. Most farmers begin to see measurable change within their first season when the practice is protected and repeated.

Step 01

Assess Your Land

Walk your fields and map existing stumps, root systems and shrub regrowth. Identify which species are most valuable for your landscape and goals.

Step 02

Select & Prune

Choose the strongest shoots on each stump and prune away the rest. Focus your farm's energy on the trees with the greatest potential.

Step 03

Protect & Guard

Shield young trees from grazing, fire and overuse during the critical early growth period. Community agreements help protect shared land.

Step 04

Monitor & Grow

Track tree growth, crop yields and canopy cover across seasons. Share lessons with neighbours and adjust pruning as trees mature.

Community Mobilisation

FMNR works best as a community

Individual farmers can begin FMNR on their own plots, but the technique reaches its full potential when adopted across an entire landscape. Faith leaders, women's groups, village elders and local government can all help protect regrowth and spread practice.

Community Adoption

Mobilise Local Leaders

Community-wide adoption protects regenerating trees from grazing and human pressure, and creates a shared sense of ownership over the land's recovery.
