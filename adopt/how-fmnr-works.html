The basics

What Is FMNR?

Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration (FMNR) is a low-cost, sustainable land restoration technique. It involves regenerating and managing trees and shrubs from stumps, roots and seeds to combat poverty, increase food production and build resilience to climate extremes — and farmers can do it without external inputs or expensive equipment.

Already in the ground

Work With Living Rootstock

Much of the world's degraded farmland was cleared by methods that leave roots in the soil. FMNR coaxes new growth from those surviving stumps and seeds — making regreening immediate and inexpensive.

Community choice

Tailored To Local Needs

Communities have the freedom to tailor FMNR to their own needs — choosing species for firewood, shade or fruit, and adapting to local climate, soil and crop conditions.

Lasting change

Restore Barren Land

Practised consistently, FMNR regenerates trees on cropland, halts desertification and reverses deforestation — reducing food insecurity for subsistence farming families.

01

Find

Rally the community to create a realistic wish list of tree species — think about what everyone needs (firewood, shade, fruit) and what's locally available. Survey the land to see how many species are present, and identify indigenous shrubs with extensive, living root systems.

  • Agree a community wish list of useful species
  • Survey the land for surviving stumps and roots
  • Identify indigenous shrubs with strong rootstock

02

Prune

Pruning is the key principle of FMNR and is essential to its success. Select the strongest stems on each stump, remove the weaker side branches, and let the chosen stems grow tall and straight. Done well, pruning channels the plant's energy into fast, strong regrowth.

  • Select the tallest, straightest stems to keep
  • Remove weaker stems and low side branches
  • Channel root energy into strong regrowth

03

Protect

Once you've selected and pruned your trees, it's crucial to protect them from fire, livestock and competing weeds — in urban settings, from traffic and other hazards too. Ideally, exclude livestock from FMNR areas for six months to a year so trees grow tall and strong enough to withstand grazing.

  • Guard regrowth from fire and grazing
  • Clear competing weeds and vegetation
  • Exclude livestock for six to twelve months

04

Grow

New branches and shoots keep growing, so prune them every two to six months. This helps the main stems grow stronger and faster — consistent care is the key. Continue to monitor and prune your trees as they grow, season after season.

  • Re-prune new shoots every two to six months
  • Keep the strongest main stems growing
  • Monitor consistently — care is everything

05

Utilise

FMNR trees can serve many purposes based on what the community needs. Community members have the freedom to tailor FMNR to their specific situation — using available resources and adapting to local climate, soil and crop conditions to draw firewood, fodder, fruit, timber and income from their trees.

  • Harvest firewood, fodder, fruit and timber
  • Diversify income across the whole year
  • Adapt continually to community needs

FMNR in practice

From "Clean Farm" To Living Landscape

Before

The "Clean Farm" Myth

Farmers once believed a good farmer was a "clean" farmer — cutting down every tree and burning crop residue. With trees belonging to the government, there was no incentive to protect them.

See the evidence →

After

Trees Working For Farmers

Today farmers leave 40–150 trees per hectare and benefit from extra income, improved crop yields and more livestock fodder. Wind speeds have dropped and, in some regions, water tables have risen.
