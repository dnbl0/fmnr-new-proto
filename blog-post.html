From Desert to Forest: Niger's Transformation

Tony Rinaudo 12 May 2026 8 min read

A quiet revolution in the Sahel

In the late 1970s and early 1980s, the Maradi region of southern Niger was in ecological crisis. Decades of clearing land for agriculture had stripped the Sahel of its tree cover, leaving soils bare and vulnerable to wind erosion. Droughts struck repeatedly. Harvests failed. Millions of people faced food insecurity. Then, almost invisibly, something began to change.

The numbers that tell the story

Over the course of four decades, farmer-managed natural regeneration spread across southern Niger — not through a top-down programme, but through farmer-to-farmer observation and adoption. Trees were no longer cut down. They were allowed to grow from the living root systems already in the soil. The results were measurable at a landscape scale.

What farmers actually did

The technique is simple in principle. Farmers stop uprooting or burning shrubs and tree stumps on their farmland. Instead, they select the healthiest shoots from each stump, prune away the rest, and protect the growing trees from grazing animals during the critical early stages. The trees provide shade that reduces soil temperature, their roots bind the soil against erosion, their leaf litter returns organic matter to depleted ground, and their canopy captures moisture. Within a few seasons, yields improve. Within a few years, the landscape changes.

"We didn't plant these trees. They were already there, waiting underground."

What made FMNR spread so rapidly in Niger was not external intervention — it was farmer observation. As neighbouring plots began showing higher yields and more resilient harvests, adoption spread organically. By the early 2000s, satellite imagery confirmed what farmers already knew: large areas of southern Niger were measurably greener than they had been two decades earlier.

A model the world is still learning from

Niger's experience with FMNR remains the largest documented dryland restoration in history. It happened at scale with minimal external funding, driven by the self-interest and ingenuity of millions of smallholder farmers. Today, the approach is being adapted and adopted across 27 countries — from Ethiopia's highlands to the drylands of Kenya, from Zambia's miombo woodlands to farmland in South Asia. The Sahel showed what was possible. The rest of the world is paying attention.

FMNR is not a silver bullet. Tenure security, market access, and supportive land governance all play a role in enabling farmer adoption. But as a low-cost, high-impact restoration technique that puts decision-making in the hands of farmers themselves, it has no peer. Niger is proof of what's possible when farmers are trusted to manage their own land.
