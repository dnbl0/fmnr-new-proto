Country overview

FMNR in Uganda

Uganda's FMNR story is driven by farmers themselves. From peer learning networks in the east to field-supported adoption in the north, communities are recording tangible improvements in water availability, soil health and crop productivity across districts.

Water

Soil and Hydrology

FMNR-restored tree roots reduce erosion and increase soil moisture. Ugandan farmers consistently report improved spring flow and reduced crop water stress as tree cover is restored across their land.

Food

Crop Yield Gains

Communities practising FMNR report consistent yield improvements within two to three seasons, driven by improved soil health, microclimate regulation and reduced wind erosion on farmland.

Community

Farmer Champions

Early FMNR adopters in Uganda become community champions — spreading practice knowledge to neighbouring villages without additional training cost, creating self-sustaining adoption networks.

01

Farmer-Led Adoption

FMNR adoption in Uganda is fundamentally farmer-led. Peer exchange visits between villages, community demonstration plots and farmer champion identification create a self-reinforcing cycle of spread that requires minimal external input to sustain.

  • Demonstration plots established on community land
  • Peer exchange visits between adopting villages
  • Farmer champion identification and coaching
  • Early yield improvements monitored and shared

02

Field Support and Evidence

Extension officer training, field guide adaptation and satellite-based vegetation monitoring provide the evidence base needed for government and donor reporting — while creating a feedback loop that improves programme quality over time.

  • Extension officer training and ongoing coaching
  • Field guides adapted to Ugandan farming context
  • Satellite vegetation monitoring across districts
  • Evidence shared with government and donor partners
