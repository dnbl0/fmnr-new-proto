The greening of the Sahel

A Country Transformed, One Tree at a Time

In the early 1980s, the farmland of Niger's Maradi region was among the most degraded on earth. Decades of clearing had stripped the land of trees, leaving crops exposed to scorching winds and shifting sand. Yet beneath that barren ground lay a living "underground forest" — and the simple practice of protecting and pruning its regrowth would go on to transform an entire nation.

The problem

A Disappearing Landscape

By the 1980s, tree cover across Niger's croplands had collapsed. Conventional tree-planting programmes failed repeatedly — seedlings withered in the heat, and farmers saw little reason to protect them.

The breakthrough

The Underground Forest

The turning point came with the discovery of an extensive network of living stumps, roots and seeds — capable of rapidly regrowing tree cover at almost no cost, simply by being protected and managed.

The result

From Project to Movement

Over a 20-year period FMNR spread largely farmer to farmer, re-vegetating 5 million hectares — a transformation achieved in one of the world's poorest countries with little outside investment.

01

The Forest Maker Arrives

In the early 1980s, agronomist Tony Rinaudo arrived in the Maradi region of Niger determined to fight back the encroaching desert. His repeated efforts to restore tree cover through conventional planting failed miserably — seedlings died, budgets ran dry, and the land kept degrading.

  • Conventional tree planting failed repeatedly
  • The Maradi region was among the most degraded on earth
  • A new approach was needed — born of that failure

02

The Underground Forest

The breakthrough came not with new seedlings but with what was already there. Rinaudo recognised that the "barren" fields were not barren at all — beneath them lay a vast "underground forest" of living stumps, roots and seeds, able to regrow rapidly and at low cost if simply protected and pruned rather than cleared.

  • Living stumps and root systems already in the ground
  • Selective pruning of regrowth instead of planting
  • Fast, low-cost restoration farmers could do themselves

03

A Change of Mindset

Adoption was painfully slow. A few farmers tried FMNR but were ridiculed, and protected trees were often cut and stolen. The shift began in 1984, when intense radio coverage of deforestation in Maradi — followed by a severe drought and famine — connected lost trees with hardship in people's minds. Through a food-for-work programme, whole districts practised FMNR for the first time.

  • 1984 radio campaign on deforestation in Maradi
  • Drought and famine reinforced the link
  • First time a whole district kept its trees standing

04

From Project to Movement

Once farmers saw the results, FMNR became standard practice and spread largely farmer to farmer. Over 20 years it reached around 250,000 hectares per year — regenerating an estimated 200 million trees across 5 million hectares of degraded farmland, and benefiting the environment while enhancing livelihoods. FMNR had moved from being a "project" to becoming a "movement".

  • 200 million trees regenerated
  • 5 million hectares re-vegetated
  • A model now practised across more than 27 countries
“

The trees were never gone. They were waiting underground — all they needed was for us to stop cutting them down.

— The story of FMNR in Niger, after Tony Rinaudo, the "Forest Maker"
