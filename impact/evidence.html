The Evidence Behind The Regreening.

FMNR's results aren't anecdotal. A growing body of peer-reviewed research, evidence briefs and independent reviews documents its effect on livelihoods, ecosystems and resilience across the drylands.

See the numbers Browse the research

What the evidence shows

Documented, Reviewed, Measured

Across two decades of studies — from semi-arid West Africa to East Africa's highlands — researchers have measured FMNR's effects on incomes, soils, food security and landscapes. The picture is consistent: a low-cost technique with compounding, lasting returns.

Livelihoods

Stronger Rural Incomes

Peer-reviewed work by Binam et al. (2015) and Weston et al. (2014) found FMNR enhances rural livelihoods in dryland West Africa — diversifying income from wood, fodder, fruit and timber across the whole year.

Ecosystems

Low-Cost, Scalable Restoration

Rinaudo, McKenzie, Huynh and Sterrett (2021) describe FMNR as a community-driven, low-cost and scalable approach to ecosystem restoration — with Chomba et al. (2020) mapping its opportunities across Sub-Saharan Africa.

Synthesis

Independent Evidence Reviews

World Vision Australia commissioned independent consultant Dr Tafadzwa Nyanhanda to produce the 2024 Evidence Gap Map and the 2025 Regreening & Livelihoods Evidence Brief — drawing the field's findings together.

01

Impact Seen From Space

The most striking evidence is visible from orbit. Satellite imagery of Niger in 1975 shows a denuded, desertifying landscape; by 2005 the same region is dense with on-farm trees. Roughly five million hectares were re-vegetated — measurable, repeatable and independently observable.

  • 1975 vs 2005 satellite comparison, Niger
  • ~5 million hectares re-vegetated
  • ~200 million trees regenerated across the Sahel

02

Measured On The Farm

On the ground, the data matches the imagery. Farmers practising FMNR now leave 40–150 trees per hectare and report increased income, improved crop yields and more livestock fodder. Wind speeds have fallen and, in some regions, water tables have risen — outcomes captured in household-level studies.

  • 40–150 trees per hectare retained on farmland
  • Higher crop yields and livestock fodder
  • Reduced wind speeds; rising water tables in places
