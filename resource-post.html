The definitive guide to practising FMNR

The FMNR Practitioner Manual is the primary reference document for field practitioners implementing Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration across dryland farming systems. It covers the full technique — from understanding root systems and tree selection through to monitoring, community engagement, and scaling. Whether you are new to FMNR or an experienced practitioner, this manual provides the depth of guidance needed to implement FMNR effectively and ethically.

Reach & impact so far

The FMNR Practitioner Manual has been used to train field agents, NGO programme staff, and government extension workers across four continents. Its principles underpin programmes that have collectively restored tens of millions of hectares of degraded farmland.

What's inside

The manual is structured to take practitioners from foundational understanding through to field implementation and programme monitoring. Each chapter builds on the last, with practical exercises, decision trees, and illustrated guides for key techniques.

  1. Understanding FMNR — Principles & Background
  2. The Biology of Regeneration — Root Systems & Coppice
  3. Selection, Pruning & Protection Techniques
  4. Community Mobilisation & Farmer Training
  5. Land Tenure, Governance & Policy Considerations
  6. Monitoring, Evaluation & Reporting

Who this manual is for

The manual is written primarily for field practitioners — agronomists, community development workers, and NGO programme staff who work directly with farming communities. It is also used extensively by government extension workers, training institutes, and programme designers who need a thorough technical reference for designing FMNR interventions at scale.

"The most practical guide to dryland restoration I've used in 20 years of field work."

The language has been deliberately kept accessible, with technical terms explained on first use. Illustrated step-by-step guides for pruning, species identification, and monitoring are designed to be used directly in the field, including in low-literacy training contexts.
