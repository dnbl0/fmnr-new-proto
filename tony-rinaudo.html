About · Founder of FMNR

Tony Rinaudo

The man who refused to give up on the land — and changed the lives of millions.

His approach

Faith + Persistence + Community

01

Believe the Impossible

When the agricultural establishment said the Sahel was beyond saving, Tony refused to accept it. He saw what others overlooked — a hidden forest waiting beneath the surface of what appeared to be dead ground.

02

Empower the Farmer

FMNR does not depend on outside experts, expensive inputs or government programs. Tony's insight was radical: the knowledge, the labour and the solution already exist within farming communities themselves.

03

Scale through Community

Real change is contagious. By working with communities rather than for them, Tony's approach spreads farmer to farmer, village to village, country to country — without losing its local roots.

In his own words

Why FMNR matters

While living in Niger Republic, I witnessed firsthand the devastating impact of deforestation and land degradation on the world's most vulnerable people — children, women and poor farm families.

FMNR was born out of the bitterness of failure using conventional reforestation methods and, I am convinced, as a result of heartfelt prayer to God to intervene. FMNR's subsequent adoption by millions of smallholder farmers transformed broken land and lives and restored hope. Today, the Niger FMNR story inspires regreening movements around the world.

My shock at seeing people suffering from avoidable hunger and poverty — combined with joy at witnessing how such a simple and low-cost intervention can build resilience and enable vulnerable people to thrive — has galvanised my focus.

Today I am as passionate as ever promoting FMNR globally in an ambassadorial capacity: creating awareness, advocating for funding, favourable policies and action, and inspiring others to take up the challenge of restoring one billion hectares of degraded land within the next decade.

— Tony Rinaudo, FMNR Founder

Three key lessons from Niger

01

The power of people to create the future they want by working with nature instead of fighting and destroying it.

02

The power of nature to self-heal if given a chance and a small helping hand.

03

The power of movements to rapidly transform landscapes and to change history.

The journey

Accelerating land restoration since 1981

1981

A young agronomist arrives in the Sahel

Tony Rinaudo arrives in the Maradi region of Niger as a missionary agronomist with Serving in Mission (SIM). The landscape is bleak — a semi-arid wasteland stripped bare by decades of over-farming, drought and population pressure. Conventional development approaches, from tree-planting campaigns to large-scale irrigation projects, were failing to reverse the tide.

1983

The underground forest discovery

During a moment of despair on a dusty road, Tony notices small green shoots emerging from what appear to be dead tree stumps. He realises these are not weeds — they are the living remnants of a vast "underground forest" suppressed for generations by farmers who were taught that trees competed with crops.

The insight is revolutionary: the root systems of ancient trees still survive across millions of hectares of degraded farmland. By selectively pruning and protecting these shoots rather than cutting them down, farmers can regenerate productive trees in months — at virtually zero cost. FMNR is born.

1990s

Niger's great green wall begins

FMNR spreads across the Maradi and Zinder regions of Niger as farmers witness their neighbours' fields transform. Satellite imagery later confirms what local communities already know: over 5 million hectares of land have been regenerated across southern Niger — one of the largest environmental transformations in African history, driven entirely by smallholder farmers.

Studies document dramatic improvements in soil fertility, crop yields, water retention and biodiversity. Food security improves for an estimated 2.5 million people. The term "The Re-greening of the Sahel" enters the scientific literature.

2000s

World Vision and global expansion

Tony joins World Vision Australia as Principal Advisor on Food Security, providing the platform and resources to scale FMNR internationally. Training programs are developed and facilitated across Ethiopia, Mali, Senegal, Burkina Faso and beyond. The World Bank and CGIAR research centres start documenting FMNR outcomes. Tony becomes the leading advocate for farmer-centred, low-cost land restoration at an international level.

2018

Right Livelihood Award — the "Alternative Nobel"

Tony receives the Right Livelihood Award in recognition of his transformative work in land restoration and food security. The award acknowledges not just the scale of FMNR's impact but the profound paradigm shift it represents: empowering communities rather than imposing external solutions. It elevates FMNR to global prominence, attracting new research, policy attention and funding.

Today

26 countries. Millions of hectares. Growing.

FMNR is now practised in 26 countries across Africa, Asia and the Pacific. Tens of millions of hectares of degraded land are in various stages of regeneration. Tony continues to travel, train and advocate — from village meetings in Ethiopia to climate conferences in Geneva. His book The Forest Underground brings the story to a global audience.

"

The trees were never dead. They were simply waiting for someone to give them permission to grow.

— Tony Rinaudo, FMNR Founder

About

Tony Rinaudo

Principal Advisor & FMNR Pioneer

Tony grew up in rural Australia with a deep love of the land. After completing his agricultural studies he moved to Niger in 1981 with his wife Liz, not knowing that a chance observation beside a dusty road would set the course for the rest of his life.

Over four decades he has dedicated himself to demonstrating that degraded land is not dead land — and that the communities who live on that land hold the keys to its restoration. His approach is rooted in humility: Tony does not impose solutions, he reveals them.

Today Tony is recognised as one of the world's leading voices on farmer-led land restoration. He is an Honorary Associate Professor at the University of New England, a Right Livelihood Laureate, and author of The Forest Underground.
